The history of the Red Cross

The Red Cross is one of the most important humanitarian organizations in the world. It began in 1863 after a Swiss man, Henry Dunant, saw many wounded soldiers on the battlefield after the Battle of Solferino. Many soldiers were lying on the ground without help. There were not enough doctors in the area, and many patients couldn’t receive treatment.

Henry Dunant organized local people in the nearby villages to help the injured soldiers. Volunteers worked in hospitals, in tents, and in small buildings near the battlefield. Nurses and doctors could clean wounds, give medicine, and treat infection. They could help soldiers with fever, pain, and serious injuries.

However, medical workers couldn’t save everyone. There were too many wounded patients, and they couldn’t always stop diseases or serious infections. Medicine was often on small tables or in simple boxes, and medical equipment was limited.

Because of these difficult conditions, Henry Dunant suggested creating an international organization to help wounded soldiers during wars. Soon many countries supported his idea, and the Red Cross organization was created. Its mission was to help the sick and wounded in hospitals, on battlefields, and in refugee camps.

Today, the Red Cross still helps people in many places around the world. Its mission is to provide medical help, prevent disease, and protect human life during wars and disasters.

Why do doctors say Hippocratic Oath?

Hippocrates was a famous Greek doctor who lived about 2,400 years ago in ancient Greece. He was born on the island of Kos around 460 BC. Many people call him the “Father of Medicine” because he helped to change the way doctors understand disease and treatment.

Before Hippocrates, people believed that diseases came from the gods or magic. When someone had a fever, a cough, or pain, they thought it was a punishment from the gods. Hippocrates had a different idea. He believed that diseases had natural causes, such as infection, bad food, dirty water, or unhealthy living conditions.

Hippocrates carefully observed his patients. He wrote about their symptoms, such as fever, headache, or cough. He tried to find the diagnosis by studying the patient’s condition. He also believed that doctors should help the body recover naturally with good food, rest, and clean surroundings.

One of Hippocrates’ most important contributions was the Hippocratic Oath. This is a special promise that many doctors make before they start their medical career. In this oath, doctors promise to help patients, to do no harm, and to treat every person with respect and care.

Doctors swear the Hippocratic Oath because Hippocrates believed that medicine is not only a science but also a moral responsibility. A doctor must always try to help the patient and never use medical knowledge to hurt people.

Hippocrates and his students wrote many medical texts that described diseases, treatments, and the role of doctors. Their ideas helped create the foundation of modern medicine. Even today, doctors around the world respect Hippocrates and follow his principles when they treat their patients.
